## Guiding Question

**What are the challenges of using chemical energy to address our energy needs?**

Chemical fuels are useful because chemical bonds store energy in compact, transportable forms. The challenge is that releasing this energy usually requires a chemical reaction, and those reactions can create pollution, greenhouse gases, cost problems, safety concerns, or infrastructure challenges. Fossil fuels, biofuels, and fuel cells all use chemical energy, but they solve different problems and create different trade-offs.

## Combustion

### Informational Text

Combustion is a chemical reaction in which a fuel reacts with oxygen and releases energy, usually as heat and light. In fuels such as hydrocarbons, complete combustion happens when there is enough oxygen for the carbon and hydrogen atoms to fully react. The typical products are carbon dioxide and water. For example, propane can burn completely according to the balanced equation `C3H8 + 5O2 -> 3CO2 + 4H2O`.

Incomplete combustion happens when there is not enough oxygen. Instead of producing only carbon dioxide and water, the reaction can also produce carbon monoxide or solid carbon particles, often seen as soot or a yellow smoky flame. Carbon monoxide is especially dangerous because it is colorless, poisonous, and interferes with the blood's ability to carry oxygen.

The main benefit of combustion is that it releases energy quickly and reliably, which is why it has powered heating, cooking, engines, and electricity generation for so long. The challenge is that combustion can produce greenhouse gases and air pollutants. Even when combustion is complete, carbon dioxide is still released if the fuel contains carbon.

## Fossil Fuels

### Informational Text

Fossil fuels include coal, oil, and natural gas. They formed over millions of years from ancient plants and animals that were buried, compressed, and changed by heat and pressure. They are considered non-renewable because they form far more slowly than humans use them. Fossil fuels are drilled, mined, refined, transported, and burned to provide electricity, heating, transportation fuels, and industrial energy.

Fossil fuels have been so widely used because they are energy-dense, reliable, and supported by huge existing infrastructure. In the United States, 2024 preliminary EIA data show that petroleum, natural gas, and coal still made up most primary energy consumption. Natural gas and coal also remained major electricity sources. This helps explain why fossil fuels are difficult to replace quickly: modern transportation, power generation, manufacturing, and heating systems were built around them.

The biggest challenge is that burning fossil fuels releases carbon dioxide and other pollutants. NASA reported that global fossil fuel carbon dioxide emissions reached record levels in 2023, and the U.S. Department of Energy states that nearly three-fourths of human-caused emissions over the past 20 years came from burning fossil fuels. Fossil fuels can provide dependable energy, but their climate and air-quality impacts make alternatives important.

## Biofuels

### Informational Text

Biofuels are fuels made from biomass, which means recently living material such as crops, plant waste, wood, algae, used cooking oil, or animal fats. Most biofuels are liquid fuels used for transportation. Ethanol is commonly blended with gasoline, while biodiesel and renewable diesel can be used in diesel engines or diesel fuel blends. Because the feedstocks can be regrown, biofuels are usually treated as renewable alternatives to fossil fuels.

Biofuels can help address energy needs because they can replace part of the gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel used in transportation. They can also use domestic or local resources, support agricultural economies, and fit into some existing engines and fuel systems. The U.S. Department of Energy explains that biomass can be converted into liquid transportation fuels such as ethanol and biodiesel, and the EIA reports that most biofuel consumption happens as a blend with petroleum products.

The challenge is that biofuels are not automatically carbon-free. Burning biofuels releases carbon dioxide, and the net climate effect depends on how the crops are grown, processed, transported, and converted into fuel. Some first-generation biofuels can compete with food production or require land, water, fertilizer, and pesticides. Advanced options such as cellulosic ethanol could use non-food plant material and require fewer inputs, but they still face technical and economic barriers.

## Fuel Cells

### Informational Text

A fuel cell converts chemical energy directly into electrical energy. In a hydrogen fuel cell, hydrogen enters the anode and oxygen from air enters the cathode. A catalyst separates hydrogen molecules into protons and electrons. The electrons travel through an external circuit, creating electric current, while the protons move through the electrolyte. At the cathode, oxygen, protons, and electrons combine to form water and heat.

Fuel cells are different from combustion because the fuel is not burned. This direct electrochemical conversion can be efficient and quiet, and if hydrogen is the fuel, the point-of-use products are electricity, water, and heat. The Department of Energy notes that fuel cells can power transportation, buildings, portable devices, and grid storage systems. This makes them attractive for clean transportation and backup power where batteries may not be the best fit.

The challenges are production, storage, cost, and infrastructure. Hydrogen must be produced, and if it is made from fossil fuels without controlling emissions, the climate benefit is reduced. Hydrogen also has low energy content by volume, so storage often requires high pressure, low temperatures, or special materials. Fuel cell systems and hydrogen fueling stations are still expensive, and GAO reports that hydrogen transport and storage infrastructure is limited.

## Summit Defense Notes

### Case 1: Fossil Fuel Challenges

Main claim: Fossil fuels can address energy needs because they are energy-dense, reliable, and already built into electricity, transportation, and industry, but they should not remain the long-term default because their combustion releases large amounts of carbon dioxide and other pollutants.

Evidence to use:

- Fossil fuels are non-renewable and include oil, coal, and natural gas.
- They are burned for electricity, heating, and transportation.
- DOE states that nearly three-fourths of human-caused emissions over the past 20 years came from burning fossil fuels.
- NASA reports that fossil fuel CO2 emissions reached record levels in 2023.

Best defense sentence:

Fossil fuels solve the short-term reliability problem, but they worsen the long-term climate problem; alternatives are needed because energy security is not useful if it creates environmental instability.

### Case 2: Biofuels

Main claim: Biofuels can reduce dependence on petroleum because they are made from biomass and can be blended into transportation fuels, but they are only a strong alternative when their full life cycle is managed carefully.

Evidence to use:

- EIA defines biofuels as liquid fuels and blending components made from biomass feedstocks.
- DOE identifies ethanol and biodiesel as common biofuels for transportation.
- EIA explains that burning biofuels still releases CO2, and net emissions depend on production methods and cultivation.
- Cellulosic biofuels could reduce some land and input problems, but technical and economic challenges remain.

Best defense sentence:

Biofuels are not a perfect replacement, but they are useful as a transition fuel because they can work with existing transportation systems while advanced feedstocks are improved.

### Case 3: Fuel Cells

Main claim: Fuel cells can address energy needs by converting chemical energy directly into electricity with water as the point-of-use product when hydrogen is used, but hydrogen production, storage, cost, and fueling infrastructure limit widespread use.

Evidence to use:

- DOE explains that hydrogen fuel cells produce electricity, water, and heat.
- DOE notes fuel cells can exceed 60% efficiency and have no smog-forming emissions at the point of operation.
- AFDC explains that hydrogen storage is difficult because hydrogen has low energy content by volume.
- GAO reports that hydrogen transport and storage infrastructure is limited and geographically constrained.

Best defense sentence:

Fuel cells are one of the cleanest chemical-energy technologies at the point of use, but they become truly sustainable only when hydrogen production and infrastructure are also clean and practical.

## Reflection Paragraph Scaffold

Chemical energy can help meet human energy needs because fuels store large amounts of energy and can be transported, stored, and released when needed. However, every fuel pathway has trade-offs. Fossil fuels are reliable and powerful, but they are non-renewable and release greenhouse gases when burned. Biofuels can reduce dependence on petroleum and use renewable biomass, but their benefits depend on land use, feedstocks, and production methods. Fuel cells can produce electricity efficiently with water as the point-of-use product when hydrogen is used, but hydrogen production, storage, and infrastructure remain major challenges. Based on the evidence, the best future energy system will probably not rely on one fuel alone; it will need a mix of cleaner fuels, better technology, and careful decisions about environmental impact.
